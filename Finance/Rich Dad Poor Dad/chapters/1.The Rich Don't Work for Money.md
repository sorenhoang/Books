### **Chapter One Summary: Lesson 1—The Rich Don’t Work for Money**

The first chapter follows nine-year-old Robert and his friend Mike as they embark on their journey to understand wealth. Robert’s biological father (**Poor Dad**) suggests that if he wants to be rich, he must learn to make money using his head. 

#### **The First Venture and a New Mentor**
Robert and Mike’s first business attempt involves "making money" literally by casting lead nickels from melted toothpaste tubes, an act Poor Dad gently explains is **counterfeiting**. Recognizing their initiative, Poor Dad suggests they learn from Mike’s father (**Rich Dad**), who is described as a man building a financial empire. 

Rich Dad offers to teach the boys, but only if they **work for him** rather than sitting in a classroom. Robert agrees to work at one of Rich Dad's convenience stores for a meager **10 cents an hour**, sacrificing his Saturday softball games. 

#### **The Lesson of the "Rat Race"**
After three weeks of boring manual labor, Robert becomes angry and feels exploited. He confronts Rich Dad, demanding a raise or threatening to quit. Rich Dad explains that this frustration is exactly what he intended to teach: **Life is the best teacher** because it "pushes you around" to show you something needs to be learned. 

Rich Dad identifies two primary emotions that trap the poor and middle class in the **"Rat Race"**:
1.  **Fear:** The fear of being without money, which drives people to work hard for a paycheck.
2.  **Greed/Desire:** The desire for the "joy" people think money can buy, which leads them to spend more as they earn more, keeping them in a cycle of debt.

The core lesson is that **"The poor and the middle class work for money. The rich have money work for them"**. Rich Dad challenges the boys to work for **nothing** to train their brains to see opportunities that others miss because they are only looking for a paycheck and job security.

#### **Finding the "Pipeline"**
While working for free, Robert and Mike notice a comic-book distributor throwing away old comics. They negotiate to keep the discarded books and open a **comic-book library** in Mike's basement. They hire Mike's sister to manage it and charge other children admission. This business generates money even when the boys are not physically present, marking their first successful experience of having **money work for them**.

***

**Analogy for the "Rat Race":**
Rich Dad compares the average worker's life to a **donkey dragging a cart** with its owner dangling a **carrot** just out of reach. The donkey is chasing an illusion (the paycheck), while the owner (the employer or government) goes where they want to go. Financial intelligence allows a person to see the "whole picture" and stop chasing the carrot.
